# Welcome Email 4 — Permission Slip

**Sequence:** Welcome
**Number:** 4 of 6
**Send delay:** Day 7
**Subject line:** You don't need a curriculum (here's your permission slip)
**Preview text:** If you've ever lain awake wondering whether it's enough

---

If you've ever lain awake at night wondering whether your kids are "getting enough," this email is for you.

I know that feeling. The 2 a.m. spiral. The mental checklist of everything they *should* know by now. The well-meaning relative who asks, "But what about math?" The voice in your head that sounds suspiciously like every report card you ever received.

The guilt is real. I get it. I had a B.Ed, a Master's in Education, 15 years in the classroom, and all the "right" credentials — and I *still* felt it. Especially in the early months, when our days looked nothing like school and I kept waiting for someone to tell me I was doing it wrong.

Then I started actually looking at what my kids were learning.

Julia spends hours at the beach building sand worlds — towers, walls, moats, creatures. She's problem-solving, experimenting with structure and weight, adjusting when things collapse, and starting over without anyone telling her to. Nobody assigned it. Nobody graded it. She just does it because she's fascinated.

Zach orders our food in Spanish, navigates unfamiliar towns, and spent an entire afternoon researching the Panama Canal — then wrote about it in French for the family blog. He's 12. No one gave him a worksheet. He just wanted to know.

These aren't "educational moments" I manufactured. They're just what happens when kids have space, time, and real things to engage with.

So here's your permission slip, from a former teacher who's seen both sides:

**If your kids are curious, asking questions, making things, and engaging with the world around them — they are learning. Full stop.**

Not "sort of" learning. Not "learning but not enough." Learning. The deep, sticky, real kind that no test can measure and no curriculum can replicate.

You don't need a scope and sequence. You need trust — in your kids, and in yourself.

Hit reply if you needed to hear this. I read every one.

Happy learning,
Amelie
