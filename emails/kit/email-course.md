## Email 1 -- Day 1: The Grocery Store Is a Classroom

**Subject line (primary):** The grocery store is a classroom
**Subject line (alt 1):** You're already teaching math (you just don't know it)
**Subject line (alt 2):** The learning hiding in your grocery run

---

Hey there,

Yesterday I sent you the Future-Ready Skills Map, and today I want to start a little experiment with you.

For the next five days, I'm going to send you one short email a day. Each one is a tiny shift in how you see your day. No homework, no worksheets, no prep. Just a different lens.

Let's start with the grocery store.

Last month, Julia (my 9-year-old) was comparing two boxes of granola bars. She flipped them both over, read the ingredients lists out loud, then told me we should get the one with "less weird stuff." She wasn't doing a health assignment. She was just shopping with me.

Meanwhile, Zach had the calculator out on my phone because I'd told him our budget was $120. He was running a tally as we went. When we hit the produce section, he weighed the apples and did the per-pound math without anyone asking him to.

Here's the thing -- none of that was a lesson. It was a Tuesday.

But look at what was happening: reading, math, budgeting, nutrition, decision-making, real-world problem solving. All in one grocery run.

We spend so much energy trying to *add* learning to our days. But it's already happening. At the store. In the kitchen. In the car. On a walk. We just don't always see it because it doesn't look like school.

That's the shift I want to offer you this week: you don't need to create more learning. You need to notice what's already there.

**Your tiny assignment for today:**

Next time you're at the store with your kids, just pause and watch. Don't teach anything. Don't point anything out. Just notice what they're already doing. Comparing things. Asking questions. Reading signs. Making choices.

That's real learning. And it was already happening before you read this email.

Talk tomorrow.

## Email 2 -- Day 2: The Argument Your Kids Just Had? That Was a Lesson.

**Subject line (primary):** That argument your kids just had? That was a lesson.
**Subject line (alt 1):** The skill your kids are practicing when they fight
**Subject line (alt 2):** Sibling conflict is a curriculum (hear me out)

---

Hey,

Let me tell you about yesterday.

Zach and Julia were building a blanket fort. It started great. Then Zach wanted to add a second level and Julia wanted to decorate the inside. Voices went up. Someone said "that's not fair." Someone else said "you always get to decide." It devolved into a door slam and ten minutes of silence.

I almost jumped in. The old teacher in me wanted to mediate, assign roles, find a compromise.

But I didn't. I waited.

After about ten minutes, Julia went back in. She said, "What if we do your second level but I get to make the flag for the top?" Zach thought about it and said, "Fine, but I'm picking the blankets."

They negotiated. Without me.

Here's what I want you to see: that argument wasn't a failure in our day. It was the day working. Conflict resolution, emotional regulation, perspective-taking, negotiation, compromise, advocacy -- these are skills adults struggle with. And your kids are practicing them every single time they disagree.

I know it doesn't feel like learning. It feels like chaos. It feels like maybe this whole homeschooling thing is falling apart because nobody can share a roll of tape.

But think about it -- where else do kids get to practice real, high-stakes negotiation with someone they love and have to live with? This is the kind of learning that no curriculum can teach.

**Your tiny reframe for today:**

Next time your kids argue (which might be in the next fifteen minutes, let's be honest), before you intervene, count to ten. Just watch. Ask yourself: what skills are they actually practicing right now?

You might be surprised.

xo, Amelie

## Email 3 -- Day 3: Why Boredom Is Your Secret Weapon

**Subject line (primary):** Why boredom is your secret weapon
**Subject line (alt 1):** What happens after "I'm bored"
**Subject line (alt 2):** Stop fixing boredom

---

Hey,

I need to tell you about the travel blog.

When we were in Costa Rica, it rained for three days straight. We were in a small Airbnb. No pool. Spotty wifi. The kids had exhausted their screen time by noon on the first day.

By the afternoon of day two, they were fully, dramatically bored.

I almost caved. I almost pulled out one of the workbooks I'd been lugging through three airports (more on that mistake another day). But something in me just... waited.

On the morning of day three, Zach said, "What if we made a blog about our trip?" Julia said she'd draw the pictures. Within an hour, they were interviewing each other about their favorite places, writing descriptions, arguing about what to include.

That blog is still going. They've posted dozens of entries. They film and edit YouTube videos to go with it. Nobody assigned it. Nobody graded it. It came from three rainy days and nothing to do.

Here's what I've learned: boredom is not a problem to solve. It's a doorway. When we rush to fill every empty moment with activities or screens or "educational" content, we close that door. We take away the chance for kids to sit with discomfort, get creative, and figure out what *they* actually want to do.

The most interesting things my kids have ever done came after boredom. Not before it.

I know it's hard. The whining is real. The guilt ("shouldn't I be doing something?") is real. But the magic on the other side of that discomfort is also real.

**Your tiny challenge for today:**

The next time your kid says "I'm bored," try this: say "I know" and walk away. Don't offer suggestions. Don't feel guilty. Just wait thirty minutes and see what happens.

It might be nothing. But it might be a travel blog.

xo, Amelie

## Email 4 -- Day 4: The One Question That Turns Any Moment Into Learning

**Subject line (primary):** The one question that changes everything
**Subject line (alt 1):** Forget lesson plans -- you need this one question
**Subject line (alt 2):** The question that works anywhere, any age

---

Hey,

I want to give you the most useful thing I've learned in eight years of teaching and four years of homeschooling. It's one question.

Not "What did you learn today?" (too much pressure.)
Not "Can you tell me about this?" (feels like a test.)
Not "What's 7 times 8?" (definitely a test.)

The question is: **"What do you notice?"**

That's it.

We were on a boat in the Pacific, off the coast of El Salvador. A humpback whale surfaced about fifty meters from us. The kids were screaming, I was crying (I'm that person), and when things calmed down, I just said: "What do you notice?"

Zach noticed that the whale blew air before diving. Julia noticed there was a smaller one swimming close -- a calf. Zach asked why they spray water. Julia wanted to know if whales sleep.

That evening, without me suggesting it, Zach was reading about humpback migration on my phone and Julia was drawing the mother and calf from memory.

All from "what do you notice?"

This question works because it has no wrong answer. It doesn't ask kids to perform or prove anything. It just invites them to look closer. And once they start looking, curiosity does the rest.

It works at the beach. It works at the mechanic's shop. It works in the backyard watching ants. It works with a two-year-old and a twelve-year-old. It works when you have zero plan for the day and need something -- anything -- to spark a conversation.

**Your challenge for today:**

Use it once. Anywhere. Doesn't matter where. Just ask "What do you notice?" and then do the hardest part -- be quiet and listen.

Let their curiosity lead. You don't need to have the answers. You just need the question.

xo, Amelie

## Email 5 -- Day 5: You're Already Doing This

**Subject line (primary):** You're already doing this
**Subject line (alt 1):** The permission slip you didn't know you needed
**Subject line (alt 2):** You were a great teacher before you read any of this

---

Hey,

We've spent four days together now. You've looked at grocery stores, sibling fights, boredom, and one simple question. And I hope something has shifted a little -- that you're starting to see learning in places you didn't expect it.

But I want to be honest with you about something.

I spent fifteen years in a classroom. I have a B.Ed and an M.Ed. I've read more pedagogy books than I can count. And when I first pulled my kids out of school, I did the most ridiculous thing: I tried to recreate school at my kitchen table.

I made lesson plans. I printed schedules. I bought workbooks. I timed subjects in 40-minute blocks. I was so deep in my own conditioning about what "real learning" looked like that I couldn't see the learning that was already happening every single day.

It took months for me to let go. To trust that Zach reading about the Panama Canal for three hours -- in French, drawing cross-section diagrams nobody asked for -- was enough. To trust that Julia sorting shells by size and color on a beach in Panama was math and science and art all at once. To trust that my kids picking up Spanish by ordering food at a restaurant was more language learning than any app could deliver.

The learning was already happening. It just didn't look like school.

And here's what I want you to hear: it was already happening for you, too. Before this email course. Before the free guide. Before you ever heard of Anywhere Learning. Every time you cooked with your kids, every time you let them wander outside, every time you answered a weird question at bedtime -- that was real, meaningful learning.

You don't need permission to trust yourself. But if you need to hear it from someone who spent fifteen years in the system and then walked away from it -- here it is:

**You are enough. What you're doing is enough. The learning is already there.**

That's what Anywhere Learning is about. Not adding more to your plate. Helping you see what's already on it.

Thank you for spending this week with me. It means more than you know.
