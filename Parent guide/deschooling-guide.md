# The Deschooling Guide

## Finding Your Family's Rhythm After Leaving School

**By Amelie, Anywhere Learning**

## A Letter From Amelie

Dear friend,

If you're holding this guide, you've probably done something brave recently. You pulled your kids out of school — or you're about to. And right now, you might be feeling a confusing mix of relief and terror. Maybe a little guilt. Probably a lot of "am I really doing this?"

I've been exactly where you are. I spent fifteen years as a classroom teacher. I have a B.Ed and an M.Ed. I understood the system inside and out — and I still felt paralyzed when I decided to homeschool my own kids. Because knowing the theory and actually living it with your own children at the kitchen table on a Tuesday morning are two very different things.

When we first left, I did exactly what most former teachers do — I started with lesson plans and worksheets. Old habits. I practically set up a classroom at our kitchen table. It took me weeks to realize I was rebuilding the exact thing we'd walked away from.

I wrote this guide because I wish someone had handed it to me during those first shaky months. Not a curriculum. Not a schedule. Just someone saying: here's what's going to happen, here's what's normal, and here's permission to trust the process even when it feels like nothing is working. That's what this is. Consider it a conversation between friends — one who's a few steps ahead on the same path.

You're going to be okay. Your kids are going to be okay. And this messy, uncertain chapter? It's the beginning of something really good.

xo, Amelie

## Chapter 1: What Deschooling Actually Is (and Isn't)

### You didn't leave school to build another one

Let's start with the thing nobody tells you when you pull your kids out of school: the hardest part isn't figuring out what to teach. It's unlearning everything you believe about what learning is supposed to look like.

That process has a name. It's called deschooling.

Deschooling is the transition period after leaving traditional school where your whole family — kids and parents — unlearns the habits, expectations, and anxieties that the school system installed. It's not a curriculum. It's not a method. It's a necessary reset.

### What deschooling is NOT

Let's clear up the biggest misconception right away: deschooling is not doing nothing. It's not lazy parenting. It's not giving up on your child's education.

Deschooling is the deliberate act of stepping away from everything that feels like school so your family can rediscover what learning actually looks like when nobody's grading it, timing it, or comparing it to a standard.

It's also not the same as unschooling (though they share some DNA). Unschooling is a long-term educational approach. Deschooling is a transition. Think of it as the bridge between the old way and whatever comes next for your family.

### It's for YOU as much as your kids

Here's the part that catches most parents off guard: you need to deschool too.

You grew up in the same system your kids just left. You spent twelve-plus years learning that education happens at a desk, on a schedule, with a teacher in charge. Those beliefs are deeply wired. They're going to surface as anxiety every time your child spends a Tuesday afternoon building with Lego instead of doing long division.

Your child's deschooling might look like sleeping in and watching YouTube. Yours will look like fighting the urge to pull out a workbook every time they seem "unproductive." Both are necessary.

### How long does it take?

The common guideline is roughly one month of deschooling for every year your child spent in traditional school. A child who was in school from kindergarten through fourth grade might need five months before they're ready to engage with self-directed learning.

That sounds like a long time. It is.

But here's what I learned from fifteen years in the classroom: rushing kids through transitions never works. It just creates resistance.

When we left, I had a small panic about what Zach and Julia were "missing." Then they spent a week at an outdoor school in Costa Rica — no walls, surrounded by jungle, kids from around the world learning together in the open air. My kids came alive in ways I hadn't seen in years. They weren't behind. They were finally in the right place. But it took months of decompression before they were open to experiences like that.

The families who give deschooling the time it needs come out the other side with curious, self-motivated kids. The ones who panic at week three and buy a boxed curriculum end up recreating the exact dynamic they were trying to escape.

Your timeline will be different from anyone else's. Some kids deschool in weeks. Some take a full year. The guideline is just that — a guideline, not a deadline.

### What you're actually unlearning

Deschooling means letting go of beliefs you probably don't even realize you hold. Things like:

- Learning must happen at a desk, during specific hours
- If it's not measurable, it's not learning
- Kids need to be taught by someone qualified
- Falling behind is a real and dangerous thing
- Boredom means something has gone wrong
- Play is separate from education
- Progress should be visible and linear

These ideas feel like facts. They're not. They're just the rules of one particular system — a system your family has chosen to leave.

Letting go of them takes time. And it happens in stages.

> Deschooling isn't a break from learning. It's the beginning of real learning.

### Reflection prompts

- What beliefs about education did you absorb from your own schooling that might be driving your anxiety right now?
- When you picture your child "learning," what does that image look like? Where did that image come from?
- What would it feel like to let go of the idea that your child needs to be "on track"?

## Chapter 2: The Five Stages of Deschooling

Every family's deschooling journey looks a little different. But after going through it myself, watching dozens of families navigate it, and drawing on my years as an educator — I've noticed a pattern. Five stages that almost every family moves through.

Knowing what's coming won't make it painless. But it will make it less terrifying. When you're deep in Stage 3 and wondering if you've ruined your children, you can come back to this chapter and remember: this is normal. This is the process. You're not failing — you're right on schedule.

### Stage 1: Relief and euphoria

**What it looks like:** The first few days feel like a vacation. No alarm clocks. No rushed mornings. No homework battles. No Sunday-night dread. Your child is visibly lighter. You might be too.

**What you feel:** Freedom. Excitement. A giddy sense of "we actually did it." Maybe some disbelief that you're really doing this. Everything feels possible.

**What to do:** Let it breathe. Enjoy it. Sleep in. Go for walks. Eat lunch whenever you feel like it. Let your child stay in pajamas until noon if they want to. This exhale is important — your family has been holding its breath for a long time.

**What NOT to do:** Don't use this energy to set up a homeschool classroom, buy a whiteboard, or create a color-coded schedule. I know it's tempting. You feel motivated and ready to build something new. But if you rush to fill this space with structure, you'll skip the rest of the process — and it will catch up with you later.

### Stage 2: Boredom and resistance

**What it looks like:** The honeymoon fades. Your child starts saying "I'm bored" twelve times a day. They might ask to go back to school — not because they loved it, but because it was familiar. They don't know what to do with unstructured time. Neither do you.

**What you feel:** Doubt. A creeping fear that you've made a mistake. The overwhelming urge to do something — anything — that looks like education. You'll be tempted to pull out a textbook just to feel like progress is happening.

**What to do:** Hold steady. Boredom is not the enemy. It's the doorway. Your child has spent years being told what to do every forty-five minutes. They've never had to generate their own direction. That skill doesn't appear overnight — it has to be built, and boredom is where the building starts.

Keep the environment rich but open. Leave books around. Have art supplies accessible. Suggest outings but don't force them. Your job right now is to be present and patient, not to be a teacher.

**What NOT to do:** Don't rescue them from boredom. Don't hand them a screen every time they're restless (some screen time is fine — panicked screen time to fill the void is different). And don't interpret their boredom as evidence that deschooling isn't working. It's evidence that it is.

> Boredom is not the enemy. It's the doorway to self-directed learning. Your child is learning to think for themselves for the first time in years.

### Stage 3: The messy middle

**What it looks like:** This is the longest stage and the hardest one. Your child has stopped resisting, but they haven't found their rhythm yet. Days feel shapeless. Some days they're engaged in something; other days they stare out the window. There's no consistency. No clear direction. It looks, from the outside, like nothing is happening.

**What you feel:** This is where most parents want to quit. The invisible learning is brutal. You can't point to anything and say "look, education is happening." There's no worksheet to show your mother-in-law. No test score to reassure you. Just a lot of open, messy, uncertain days.

The guilt hits hard in this stage. Every parenting voice in your head is screaming that you're wasting your child's time. That they're falling behind. That you should be doing more.

**What to do:** This is the most important thing I can tell you: stay the course. The messy middle is where the real transformation happens. Your child is detoxing from years of extrinsic motivation — grades, stickers, teacher approval. They're learning to be motivated by their own curiosity instead of someone else's schedule. That's a fundamental rewiring, and it doesn't happen fast.

Keep observing. Write down what you notice, even tiny things. Your child spent an hour watching a documentary about octopuses? That counts. They organized their entire bookshelf by color? That's categorization, planning, and decision-making. They argued with their sibling about the rules of a card game they invented? That's negotiation, logic, and creative thinking.

During our messy middle, I remember watching Julia spend an entire morning sorting shells on a beach. Just sorting. By size, by color, by type. My old teacher brain almost said "okay, let's make this into a math lesson." Instead I sat down and sorted with her. She told me about each one. She knew more about them than I did. That was learning — deep, focused, self-directed — and it looked like playing in the sand.

The learning is happening. It just doesn't look like school — and that's the whole point.

**What NOT to do:** Don't panic-buy a curriculum. Don't start testing them to see if they're "keeping up." Don't compare your messy middle to someone else's polished Instagram homeschool. And please, don't give up. This stage breaks people because it's uncomfortable, not because it's failing.

### Stage 4: Sparks of curiosity

**What it looks like:** One morning, your child asks a question. A real question — not "is this for a grade?" but "why do volcanoes happen?" or "how do they make video games?" They pick up a book without being asked. They start a project. They spend three hours building something and don't want to stop for dinner.

These sparks are small at first. Easy to miss if you're not watching. But they're the clearest sign that deschooling is working.

**What you feel:** Hope. Relief. Maybe tears. The sparks feel like proof that your child's curiosity wasn't destroyed — it was just buried under years of being told what to care about and when.

I'll never forget the moment Zach became obsessed with the Panama Canal. We'd visited Miraflores Locks and he just... couldn't stop. He researched the three lock systems, calculated that over 14,000 boats pass through per year, found out that roughly 20,000 workers died during the French attempt. He drew diagrams. He wrote a research piece — in French — for the family blog. Nobody assigned any of it. That's what a spark looks like.

**What to do:** Notice without smothering. This is critical. When your child gets excited about something, your instinct will be to turn it into a Unit. Don't. Don't buy the curriculum. Don't print the worksheets. Don't create a "volcano study plan."

Instead, follow their lead. If they're into volcanoes, go to the library and grab five books about volcanoes. Watch a documentary together. Look up videos of eruptions. Maybe visit a natural history museum. Let the interest breathe and grow on its own.

If it fizzles after two days, that's fine. Another spark will come. Julia went through a phase where all she wanted to do was draw posters about hurricanes and sea turtles. I almost interrupted it to do "real" school. I'm so glad I didn't — those posters became the thumbnails for YouTube videos she scripted, filmed, and edited herself. The point isn't to build a semester around every interest — it's to show your child that their curiosity matters and that nobody is going to turn their joy into an assignment.

**What NOT to do:** Don't test them on what they've learned. Don't ask them to write a report about it. Don't say "see, we can make this into school!" The fastest way to extinguish a spark is to turn it into homework.

### Stage 5: A new rhythm

**What it looks like:** There's no graduation ceremony for this stage. You just notice, one week, that things feel different. Your family has a rhythm. It doesn't look like school. It doesn't look like what you imagined. But it works.

Your child has interests they pursue. They ask questions. They're willing to tackle hard things — not because someone made them, but because they chose to. The daily battles about learning are gone. The anxious knot in your stomach has loosened.

**What you feel:** Settled. Not perfect — homeschooling is never effortless. But grounded. You trust your child's capacity to learn. You trust yourself to facilitate it. The foundation feels solid, even on hard days.

**What to do:** Celebrate quietly. Look back at where you started and notice how far you've come. This rhythm will keep evolving — it's not a destination, it's a living thing. Stay curious alongside your kids. Keep observing. Keep following the sparks.

**What NOT to do:** Don't assume this means everything is figured out forever. You'll have rough weeks. Your rhythm will need adjusting as your kids grow. That's not a setback — it's normal. Flexibility is the whole point.

> Deschooling isn't doing nothing. It's doing the most important thing: letting your child remember that learning belongs to them.

### A note about timelines

These stages aren't neat boxes. Your child might bounce between Stage 2 and Stage 3 for weeks. You might see sparks of Stage 4 and then slip back into the messy middle. That's completely normal.

And if you have more than one child, they will almost certainly move through these stages at different speeds. Your seven-year-old might fly through deschooling in two months while your eleven-year-old takes six. This doesn't mean anything is wrong with either of them. It just means they're different humans processing a big change in different ways.

### Reflection prompts

- Which stage do you think your family is in right now? Which stage are YOU in?
- What would it look like to give this process more time instead of more structure?
- Can you name one spark of curiosity you've noticed in your child this week, no matter how small?

## Chapter 3: Deschooling Yourself (The Parent's Work)

Here's the truth nobody prepares you for: your child will probably deschool faster than you will.

Kids are resilient. Given space and safety, they bounce back toward curiosity naturally. But you? You've had decades of conditioning. You've been marinating in beliefs about education since you were five years old, and those beliefs don't dissolve just because you've read a few blog posts about unschooling.

This chapter is about you. It's the work that makes everything else possible.

### The guilt is not a signal

Let me say this clearly: the guilt you're feeling is not a sign that you're doing something wrong. It's a sign that you're unlearning what school taught you about learning.

Every time your child plays for three hours instead of doing math, a voice in your head says "this isn't enough." Every time someone asks what grade they're in and you fumble the answer, your stomach drops. Every time your mother says "but what about their education?" you feel a wave of shame.

That guilt is coming from your schooling, not your child's behavior. You were taught that learning must be structured, measurable, and directed by an authority figure. When your homeschool doesn't look like that, your nervous system reads it as danger.

It's not danger. It's freedom. And it takes time for your body to learn the difference.

### Beliefs to examine

I'm not going to tell you to just "let go" of your beliefs about education. That's not how deeply held beliefs work. Instead, I want you to look at them. Name them. Question them. Decide consciously whether you still want to carry them.

Here are some of the big ones:

**"Learning must be structured to be real."**

Ask yourself: where did you learn the most in your own life? Was it in a classroom, or was it through experience, conversation, failure, and curiosity? Most adults, when they're honest, learned their most valuable skills outside of school. Yet we insist that children can only learn inside a structure. Why?

**"My kids need to be taught. They can't learn on their own."**

Your child learned to walk, talk, and navigate complex social dynamics before they ever set foot in a classroom. Children are wired to learn. What they need from you is not instruction — it's environment, access, and presence.

**"If we're not doing something, we're wasting time."**

This one is sneaky because it feels productive. But rest is not waste. Play is not waste. Staring out the window is not waste. Some of the most important cognitive work happens when kids look like they're doing nothing. Boredom is where creativity starts. Daydreaming is where connections form. Downtime is where everything they've absorbed gets processed and integrated.

**"My child is falling behind."**

Behind whom? Behind a standardized timeline that assumes every child learns the same things at the same speed? That timeline was designed for crowd management, not for individual children. Your child isn't on anyone else's timeline anymore. They're on their own.

**"I'm not qualified to do this."**

You don't need a teaching degree to homeschool. I have two education degrees, and honestly? The most important thing I brought to homeschooling wasn't my credentials. It was my willingness to sit with my kids, pay attention to what they were interested in, and get out of the way.

I used to worry that my kids weren't producing enough "work." That their education didn't have enough tangible evidence. Then I looked at what they'd actually created in a few months: research pieces in French, YouTube presentations with original illustrations, blog posts, hand-drawn maps, species identification charts, and hours of conversation where they taught me things I didn't know. None of it was assigned. All of it was real. You already have what your kids need — you just have to trust yourself enough to see it.

### Your permission slips

I used to joke that what homeschool parents really need isn't a curriculum — it's a stack of permission slips. So here are yours. Tear them out. Stick them on your fridge. Read them when you're panicking at 2am.

**You have permission to skip the textbook.** Your child can learn history through stories, documentaries, and visiting places. They can learn science through baking, gardening, and asking questions. They can learn math through cooking, budgeting, and building. The textbook is one tool, not the only tool — and often not the best one.

**You have permission to follow their interests.** If your child wants to spend two weeks learning everything about sharks, let them. That's not a distraction from "real learning" — it IS real learning. Deep dives into passionate interests build research skills, reading comprehension, critical thinking, and intrinsic motivation.

**You have permission to count the real stuff.** A nature walk is science. Baking is math. A trip to the grocery store is economics. Building a fort is engineering. An argument with a sibling is conflict resolution. Playing a board game is strategy, logic, and social skills. Zach practiced fractions figuring out tip percentages at restaurants. Julia learned about marine ecosystems by actually seeing tide pools, not reading about them. Both kids picked up Spanish by ordering their own food and chatting with neighbors — no flashcards required. You don't need to formalize it. You just need to trust that it counts — because it does.

**You have permission to have bad days.** Not every day will feel meaningful. Some days you'll all be cranky and screen-addled and eating cereal for dinner. That's fine. One bad day doesn't undo the good ones. One bad week doesn't mean homeschooling has failed.

**You have permission to not have it figured out.** You don't need a five-year plan. You don't need to know what curriculum you'll use in third grade when your child is in first. You need to know what's working right now, and that's enough.

### Deschooling yourself in practice

So what does your deschooling actually look like, day to day?

It looks like catching yourself about to say "stop playing and come do your schoolwork" — and choosing not to.

It looks like sitting with the discomfort of an unstructured afternoon instead of reaching for a workbook.

It looks like noticing when you're comparing your child to their schooled peers and gently redirecting your thoughts.

It looks like letting your child be bored without rushing in to fix it.

It looks like going to bed without having "accomplished" anything educational, and being okay with that.

It's uncomfortable. It's slow. And it's some of the most important work you'll do as a homeschooling parent.

> The guilt isn't a sign you're doing it wrong. It's a sign you're unlearning what school taught you about learning.

### Reflection prompts

- What's the belief about education that's hardest for you to let go of? Where did it come from?
- What would change in your daily life if you truly believed that play counts as learning?
- Write yourself a permission slip. What do you need permission to do (or stop doing) right now?

## Chapter 4: What to Actually DO During Deschooling

Okay. So you understand what deschooling is. You know the stages. You're working on your own unlearning. But you still wake up every morning and think: "What are we supposed to DO all day?"

The answer is simpler than you expect. And it's less than you think.

### A gentle week-by-week framework

I'm calling this a framework, not a schedule. There are no times attached. No checkboxes. No requirements. Just a gentle rhythm to hold onto when the days feel shapeless. Adapt it, ignore parts of it, throw the whole thing out if it doesn't fit. It's a suggestion, not a prescription.

**Week 1: Decompress.**

Do nothing structured. Sleep in. Stay in pajamas. Go outside when the weather is good. Read if you feel like it. Watch a movie in the middle of the afternoon. Eat when you're hungry, not when a bell tells you to.

This week is about exhaling. Your family has been running on someone else's schedule for years. Before you can build a new rhythm, you need to let the old one fully dissolve.

If you feel panicky about "wasting a week" — that's your own deschooling talking. Sit with it.

**Week 2: Observe.**

Start paying attention — without directing. What does your child reach for when nobody's telling them what to do? What questions do they ask? What do they talk about at dinner? What do they watch, build, draw, argue about?

Keep a little notebook (for your eyes only) and jot down what you notice. Not to grade or evaluate — just to see. These observations will become your compass later.

This is also a good week to notice your own patterns. When do you feel anxious? What triggers the urge to "do school"? What does your ideal day actually look like, separate from what you think it should look like?

**Week 3: Follow one thread.**

If something has caught your child's attention — a topic, a question, an activity — follow it gently. Not as a lesson. Just as an exploration.

If they've been asking about space, go to the library and grab a stack of space books. If they've been building elaborate structures with household objects, get some new materials and step back. If they've been fascinated by cooking shows, make dinner together.

You're not creating a unit study. You're showing your child that their interests matter enough to be taken seriously.

**Week 4: Notice the rhythm.**

By now, patterns are starting to emerge — theirs, not yours. Maybe your child is a late riser who does their best thinking in the afternoon. Maybe they need outdoor time first thing or they're restless all day. Maybe they gravitate toward hands-on projects, or they're happiest when they're reading.

These patterns are the beginning of your family's rhythm. They'll keep shifting and evolving, but the fact that they're emerging at all means something is clicking.

**Month 2 and beyond: Keep going.**

Deschooling doesn't end on a specific date. It fades gradually. You'll notice that the anxious days get further apart. That your child's sparks of curiosity get brighter and more frequent. That you're reaching for the workbook less and trusting the process more.

Keep observing. Keep following interests. Keep resisting the urge to formalize everything. The rhythm gets clearer with time.

### Simple activities that support deschooling

These aren't lessons. They're invitations. Things you can offer (not assign) that support the deschooling process without turning it into school. None of them require prep. All of them count as learning, even if they don't feel like it.

**Nature walks with no agenda.** Not a scavenger hunt. Not a leaf identification lesson. Just go outside and walk. Let your child lead. Stop when they want to stop. Look at what they want to look at. If they want to throw rocks in a puddle for forty-five minutes, that's the walk. Walking a beach one morning, my kids noticed the cliffs crumbling and wanted to understand erosion. In a park, we saw whales from a boat and spent the evening reading about migration patterns. None of it was planned. It just happened because we were outside with no agenda. You don't need to make it educational. It already is.

**Cooking together.** Pick a recipe. Read it together. Measure ingredients. Talk about what happens when you add heat to butter or why bread needs time to rise. This is reading, math, science, sequencing, and life skills — and nobody has to call it that.

**Building something.** Anything. A blanket fort. A cardboard castle. A birdhouse from a YouTube tutorial. A Lego city. Building activates spatial reasoning, planning, problem-solving, and persistence. Let them build whatever they want, however they want, without correcting their approach.

**Reading aloud.** Even to older kids. Especially to older kids. Curl up on the couch and read something together. A novel. A graphic novel. A nonfiction book about something weird. Read-aloud time is one of the most powerful learning activities that exists, and it costs nothing but time. It also happens to be a beautiful way to reconnect after a hard transition.

**Board games and card games.** Strategy, logic, math, turn-taking, losing gracefully, winning humbly — games teach all of this and more. Play together as a family. Let your kids choose the games. Don't turn it into a lesson. Just play.

**Free journaling or drawing.** Put a journal and some pens on the table. No prompt. No requirement. If they use it, great. If they don't, also great. Some kids process the deschooling transition through writing or drawing, and having the tools available without pressure is all they need.

**Exploring the neighborhood.** Walk to a part of your area you've never been to. Visit a shop you've always driven past. Talk to the person at the farmer's market about how they grow their tomatoes. Let your child discover that learning happens everywhere — not just in buildings labeled "school."

**Following a YouTube rabbit hole together.** Start with a video about something your child is curious about. Let the algorithm take you on a journey. Talk about what you're learning. Pause and look things up. Follow the tangents. This is research. It's just research that looks like fun.

**Sketching what you see.** Sit outside with a pencil and paper and draw whatever is in front of you. No skill required. No art lesson. Just looking closely at the world and putting it on paper. Observation is one of the foundational skills of science, art, and writing — and it starts with slowing down enough to really see.

**Making something to give away.** Bake cookies for a neighbor. Write a letter to a grandparent. Make a card for a friend. Creating something for someone else builds empathy, purpose, and the understanding that skills are for real use — not just for grades.

### When you're panicking and "nothing is happening"

It will happen. Probably more than once. You'll have a day — or a week — where everything feels wrong. Your child has been watching YouTube for three days straight. They haven't touched a book in a month. Your neighbor's kid is doing multiplication tables and your kid is building a pillow fort.

When the panic hits, try this:

**First, breathe.** Literally. Slowly. The panic is your old programming firing, not an actual emergency.

**Then, zoom out.** Think about the last month, not the last day. Has your child laughed? Asked a question? Made something? Argued passionately about something they care about? Learned a new skill, even a small one? If yes — learning is happening. It just doesn't look like what you were trained to recognize.

**Then, write it down.** Make a list of everything your child has done, explored, created, or shown interest in over the past two weeks. I guarantee the list is longer than you think. We're trained to see learning only when it looks like school. When you start looking for it everywhere, you'll find it everywhere.

**Then, talk to someone who gets it.** Another homeschool parent. An online community. Someone who won't respond to your fear with "see, I told you this was a bad idea." You need someone who will say: "Yes, that's normal. You're doing fine."

**And if you still feel panicky?** Go outside. Movement and fresh air don't solve everything, but they break the cycle of anxious thinking. Take your kids with you. Nobody ever came back from a long walk thinking "we should have stayed inside and done worksheets."

### Reflection prompts

- What did your child do today that you would never have seen in a classroom?
- If you made a list of everything your child has learned this month — not just academics, but all of it — what would be on it?
- What's one thing you could stop doing this week that would give your family more breathing room?

## Chapter 5: Handling the Hard Parts

Deschooling would be easy if it existed in a vacuum. But it doesn't. It exists inside a family, inside a community, inside a culture that has very strong opinions about what education is supposed to look like.

This chapter is about the hard parts that aren't really about deschooling at all — they're about navigating the people and situations around it.

### When your partner isn't on board

This is more common than anyone talks about. One parent is ready to throw out the textbooks. The other is terrified the kids will end up "behind" or uneducated or socially stunted.

First: your partner's fear is valid. They're not being difficult. They're processing the same cultural conditioning you are — they just haven't had as much time to question it.

Here's what worked for us and for many families I've talked to:

**Propose a trial period.** "Let's try three months and see what happens" is much less scary than "we're homeschooling forever." It gives your partner an off-ramp, which makes them more willing to try.

**Address their specific fear.** If they're worried about socialization, make a plan for social activities. If they're worried about math, agree to check in on math skills after the trial. Don't dismiss the fear — work with it.

**Let the kids be the evidence.** Most resistant partners come around when they see the change in their kids. When the morning battles disappear. When their child starts voluntarily reading. When the anxiety lifts. The evidence is usually more convincing than any argument.

**Don't make it a war.** You're on the same team. You both want what's best for your kids — you just disagree about what that looks like right now. Keep the conversation open. Share articles and podcasts gently, not as weapons.

### When family questions your choices

"But what about socialization?"
"How will they get into college?"
"Aren't you worried they'll fall behind?"
"I just think kids need structure."

You will hear these. Probably from people you love. And they will sting, because underneath every question is an unspoken judgment: you're not doing enough.

Here's what I want you to know: you don't owe anyone a defense of your educational choices. You're the parent. But if you want to respond (and sometimes you do, for your own sanity), here are some approaches that work:

**For the socialization question:** "They socialize through co-ops, sports, community activities, neighborhood friends, and family. Honestly, they interact with a wider range of ages and people than they did at school."

**For the falling-behind question:** "Behind what standard? Kids develop at different rates, and we're focusing on deep understanding rather than keeping pace with a standardized timeline."

**For the structure question:** "We have structure — it just looks different from a classroom. We have routines, regular activities, and plenty of learning. It's just built around our family instead of around a school schedule."

**For the persistent critic:** "I appreciate that you care about the kids. We've thought about this a lot, and we're happy with how things are going. I'd love for you to see what they're working on — maybe you could join us for a nature walk / project / library trip sometime?"

Inviting people in is almost always more effective than arguing with them. When grandma sees your child excitedly explaining how volcanoes work, it's worth a thousand conversations about whether homeschooling is valid.

And for the people who will never come around? You're allowed to set a boundary. "We've made our decision, and I'm not looking for input on this. Let's talk about something else."

### When your kid says "I'm bored"

This is going to happen constantly in the first few weeks. And every time, you'll feel a jolt of panic. "SEE? They need MORE. I should be DOING something."

Breathe. Then remember: boredom is the bridge to curiosity.

Your child has spent years in an environment where every moment was filled for them. They've never had to generate their own engagement. That's a skill, and like all skills, it develops through practice.

When your child says "I'm bored," they're really saying: "I don't know what to do with this freedom yet." That's not a problem. It's a stage.

You don't need to fix it. You can acknowledge it ("Yeah, it takes a while to figure out what to do with all this time"), offer a few options without pressure ("The art supplies are out, or we could take a walk — or you could just hang out for a while"), and then... let it sit.

The magic happens in the gap between "I'm bored" and whatever they do next. That gap is where self-direction is born.

### When YOU want to quit

There will be a day — maybe a week, maybe a month — when you are done. When every homeschool decision feels wrong. When you're exhausted and overwhelmed and your child is being difficult and the house is a mess and you haven't had a conversation with another adult in three days.

This is burnout, and it's real, and it doesn't mean homeschooling has failed.

Here are the signs to watch for:
- You're dreading every day, not just the occasional hard one
- You're snapping at your kids over small things
- You've lost all enthusiasm for activities you used to enjoy together
- You're fantasizing about putting them back in school, not because it's right for them, but because you need a break
- You feel like you're failing at everything — parenting, educating, keeping the house together, all of it

If this is you, here's your permission slip: **you're allowed to have a bad season.** You're allowed to take a week off. You're allowed to simplify radically. You're allowed to ask for help.

Put the books away. Turn on a movie. Order takeout. Call a friend. Take a walk by yourself if someone else can watch the kids for an hour. This is not quitting. This is sustainability.

The families who homeschool long-term are not the ones who push through burnout. They're the ones who recognize it early and take care of themselves. You can't facilitate your child's learning if you're running on empty.

### When one kid deschools faster than another

If you have more than one child, prepare for this: they will not deschool at the same pace. And the difference can be stark.

Your younger child might bounce through the stages in six weeks, happily drawing and exploring and asking a million questions. Meanwhile, your older child is still on the couch, still resistant, still saying "this is stupid."

I saw this with my own two. Julia, at 9, adapted faster — she was drawing, exploring, asking questions within weeks. Zach, at 12, had more years of school habits to shake. He needed longer to stop waiting for someone to tell him what to do next. Both got there. Just on different timelines.

This is normal. Older children have had more years of conditioning. They've internalized more rules about what counts as learning. They have stronger social identities tied to school. It takes them longer to let go of all that.

Don't compare your children to each other. Don't say "your sister is doing great — why can't you be more like her?" Don't use the faster deschooler as proof that the slower one is doing something wrong.

Each child's timeline is their own. Trust it.

### Reflection prompts

- Who in your life is the hardest to talk to about homeschooling? What specific fear are they expressing?
- When was the last time you took care of yourself — not your kids, not your home, just you?
- If one of your children is deschooling more slowly, what might they need from you that's different from what the other child needs?

## Chapter 6: How to Know Deschooling Is Working

This might be the chapter you've been waiting for. The one where I tell you the signs. The proof. The evidence that this whole terrifying experiment is actually working.

I want to be honest with you: the signs are subtle. There's no test score. No report card. No standardized assessment that says "congratulations, your child has successfully deschooled." The evidence shows up in small, quiet moments — and you have to be watching for it.

### Signs to look for

**They start asking questions again.** Real questions. Not "will this be on the test?" but "why is the sky actually blue?" or "how does Wi-Fi work?" or "what would happen if dinosaurs were still alive?" Questions driven by genuine curiosity, not by trying to get the right answer. This is huge. When a child starts asking questions for the pure joy of wondering, something essential has shifted.

**They pick up a project on their own.** They decide to build something, draw something, write something, make something — without being assigned to. It might be small. A sketch. A fort. A recipe they want to try. The key is that it came from them.

**They stop asking "is this for school?"** When your child stops categorizing activities as "school" or "not school" and just starts... doing things... that's a sign the old framework is dissolving. They're no longer dividing their life into "learning time" and "free time." It's all just life. My kids now spend their afternoons writing blog posts, making YouTube videos with hand-drawn illustrations, and researching whatever they're curious about — and neither of them thinks of it as "school." It's just what they do.

**Arguments about learning decrease.** If your early weeks involved battles about doing schoolwork, and those battles have stopped — not because you gave up, but because the dynamic has genuinely shifted — that's deschooling at work.

**They get bored AND find something to do about it.** In Stage 2, your child says "I'm bored" and waits for you to fix it. When deschooling is working, they say "I'm bored" and then, after a stretch of nothing, they find something. That transition from passive boredom to active self-direction is the whole ballgame.

**They show you something they're proud of.** Not for a grade. Not for approval. Just because they made something and they want to share it. This is intrinsic motivation in action.

**You feel less anxious.** Your deschooling is working too. You notice that you can watch your child play for an hour without a voice in your head screaming about math facts. You trust the process a little more each week. The knot in your stomach is loosening.

### Signs it might need more time

Sometimes deschooling takes longer than expected. That's okay. Here are some signs your family might need more time:

- Your child is still highly resistant to any suggestion, even gentle ones
- They seem genuinely depressed or withdrawn (not just resting — actually shutting down)
- They express a lot of anger about school or about leaving school
- You haven't seen any sparks of curiosity at all, even tiny ones

If any of these persist for several months, it might be worth talking to a counselor — not because something is wrong with your child, but because sometimes kids carry wounds from school that need more support than deschooling alone can provide. Anxiety, bullying, learning differences that went unaddressed — these can all make deschooling harder.

That said: in most cases, more time is the answer. Not more intervention. Not more structure. Just more time, more patience, and more trust.

### "Behind" is a school concept

I need to say this explicitly because it's the fear that keeps more parents awake at night than any other: **there is no behind in real life.**

"Behind" only exists inside a system that moves every child through the same content at the same pace. Once you leave that system, the concept evaporates. Your eight-year-old isn't "behind in reading" — they're developing reading skills at their own pace. Your ten-year-old isn't "behind in math" — they haven't needed those specific skills yet, and when they do, they'll learn them.

Children learn what they need when they need it. A child who can't do long division at age nine but can at age twelve hasn't "caught up" — they've just arrived at that skill on their own timeline. And they'll understand it better because they came to it when they were ready, not when a schedule said they should.

I know this is hard to believe. I know it goes against everything you've been told. But I watched it happen in my classroom for fifteen years: the kids who were "behind" in second grade often leapt ahead in fourth. The kids who were "advanced" often plateaued. Development is not linear, and treating it like a race helps no one.

Your child is not behind. They're right where they need to be.

> "Behind" is a school concept. There is no behind in a life built around curiosity.

- What would it mean for your daily life if you truly let go of the idea of "falling behind"?
- How has YOUR relationship with learning changed since you started this process?

## Chapter 7: What Comes After

Here's the good news: you don't need to have the next chapter figured out right now.

Deschooling is not a waiting room where you sit until it's time to pick a curriculum. It's a transformation. And when it's done — or rather, when it's done enough — what comes next will be clearer than you expect.

### You don't need to pick a curriculum

I know the homeschool internet makes it seem like choosing a curriculum is the first and most important decision you'll make. It's not. The most important decision was the one you already made: to leave a system that wasn't working and build something better.

Some families thrive with a structured curriculum. Some families thrive with no curriculum at all. Most families end up somewhere in the middle. And almost nobody sticks with the first thing they try.

If and when you're ready to add more structure, let your child's interests and your family's rhythm guide the choice — not a review blog or a Facebook group. What works for someone else's family may be completely wrong for yours.

### Finding YOUR family's approach

There are many approaches to home education, and they all have something to offer. Here's a quick overview — not to help you choose right now, but to show you that the options are wide open:

**Charlotte Mason** emphasizes living books, nature study, short lessons, and rich exposure to ideas. It's gentle, literature-heavy, and well-suited to families who love reading together and spending time outdoors.

**Montessori** focuses on self-directed activity, hands-on learning, and prepared environments. It works beautifully for younger children and for families who like tangible, manipulative-based learning.

**Unschooling** trusts children to direct their own education entirely, with parents as facilitators rather than teachers. It's the most radical departure from traditional schooling and requires a deep trust in your child's capacity to learn.

**Classical education** follows a three-stage model (grammar, logic, rhetoric) and emphasizes language, history, and critical thinking. It's the most structured approach and appeals to families who enjoy depth and rigor.

**Real-world learning** uses everyday experiences — cooking, building, budgeting, exploring — as the foundation of education. It's hands-on, practical, and inherently flexible.

**Eclectic** is what most families actually end up doing: pulling from multiple approaches based on what works for each child and each season. A little Charlotte Mason nature study here. Some unschooling principles there. A math curriculum when it's needed. Freedom when it's not.

The approach that works best is the one your family actually enjoys doing. If it feels like a slog every day, it's not the right fit — no matter how many bloggers recommend it.

### The rhythm looks different for every family

Some families have structured mornings and free afternoons. Some families do most of their learning on Tuesdays and Thursdays. Some families learn in bursts — two intense weeks followed by a week of rest. Some families barely distinguish between "school time" and "life."

All of these are valid. Your rhythm will emerge from your family's needs, your children's ages, your living situation, and your own energy levels. It will change over time. It should change over time.

The only rhythm that doesn't work is one that makes everyone miserable.

### Resources that support without controlling

When you're ready to add some structure or find new activities, look for resources that support your family's rhythm rather than replacing it. Things that give you ideas and starting points, not rigid lesson plans.

Open-ended activity guides — the kind where you download them, open them on any device, and follow along at your own pace — can be a lifesaver on days when you need a spark of inspiration but don't want to plan something from scratch. The best ones are flexible enough to adapt to different ages and abilities, and they're designed to be used again and again as your kids grow.

Whatever you choose, look for resources that feel like tools in your hands, not systems you have to serve.

### A final truth about "after"

There is no clean line between deschooling and whatever comes next. The transition is gradual. One day you'll realize you've been doing "the next thing" for weeks without noticing. Your child has interests. You have a rhythm. Learning is happening — naturally, organically, in the way it was always meant to.

That's deschooling working. Not with a bang. With a quiet, beautiful shift.

### Reflection prompts

- What kind of learner is your child? What approach might honor that?
- What does a "good day" look like for your family right now — not what should it look like, but what actually feels good?
- What's one thing you want to let go of as you move into this next chapter?

## A Closing Letter

Dear friend,

If you've read this far, I want you to know something: the fact that you're here — reading, thinking, worrying, trying — means you're a good parent. Not a perfect one. There's no such thing. But a good one. One who cares enough to question the default path and brave enough to try something different.

I remember the early days of our own deschooling like it was yesterday. The panic when my kids spent three days doing "nothing." The guilt when I compared our life to what their friends were doing in school. The moment Julia picked up a sketchbook and drew for two hours straight and I realized — with tears I wasn't expecting — that she hadn't voluntarily drawn anything in over a year. School had squeezed it out of her.

That was the moment I knew we were on the right track. Not because everything was perfect. Not because I had a plan. But because my child was doing something for the pure joy of it, and nobody was going to grade it.

Now, Zach (12) writes longer research posts and is getting into editing his own YouTube videos. Julia (9) draws illustrations for her blog entries and designs hand-drawn posters for their video thumbnails. They read, they create, they argue about ideas over dinner. They interact with people of all ages — from the elderly woman who runs a corner shop to kids from six different countries. That's richer learning than any single classroom could offer.

Your moment will come too. It might look different from mine. It might be quieter. It might take longer. But it will come.

In the meantime, be gentle with yourself. Be gentle with your kids. Let the process be messy. Let the days be imperfect. Let the learning be invisible sometimes.

You're not behind. Your kids are not behind. You're exactly where you need to be.

xo, Amelie
