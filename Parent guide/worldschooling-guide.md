# The Worldschooling Starter Guide

## How to Learn Everywhere You Go

**By Amelie, Anywhere Learning**

## A Letter From Amelie

Dear friend,

When I first told people we were going to travel and homeschool at the same time, most of them thought I was out of my mind.

I was a classroom teacher with fifteen years of experience. I had a B.Ed and an M.Ed. I understood child development, pedagogy, differentiated instruction — all of it. And still, the idea of taking my two kids out of school and onto a plane with no lesson plans felt reckless.

But we did it. Seven months. Florida, Panama, Costa Rica, El Salvador. My son Zach was ten, my daughter Julia was seven, and my husband and I had no idea what we were doing. We just knew that the life we were living didn't match the values we kept talking about at the dinner table.

I'm not going to tell you it was easy. I packed workbooks. I made spreadsheets. I lugged a bag of "school supplies" through three airports before I finally admitted that nobody was opening them. I spent the first few weeks panicking every time my kids weren't producing visible work.

And then something shifted.

Zach became obsessed with the Panama Canal. Not because anyone assigned it — because he saw it. He researched how it worked for weeks. He wrote about it. He drew engineering diagrams. He calculated water volumes. Julia started a nature journal, sketched every animal she saw, and spent an entire afternoon sorting seashells on a beach in Costa Rica, completely absorbed in patterns and categories.

The learning was already happening. It just didn't look like school.

That realization changed everything for our family. It's why I never went back to the classroom. And it's why I wrote this guide — not to convince you to do exactly what we did, but to show you that learning everywhere you go is possible, practical, and closer than you think.

You don't need a plane ticket to worldschool. You don't need to sell your house. You don't need to be fearless.

You just need permission to see the world as the most extraordinary classroom your kids will ever have.

This guide is that permission.

xo, Amelie

## Chapter 1: What Worldschooling Actually Is (And What It Isn't)

### It's simpler than you think

If you've spent any time searching "worldschooling" online, you've probably seen a very specific image: tanned families on beaches, kids doing math in hammocks, Instagram grids full of passport stamps and sunsets. It looks beautiful. It also looks completely unattainable for most families.

Let me tell you what worldschooling actually is, stripped of the aesthetic.

Worldschooling is a mindset. It's the belief that the world itself — not a textbook, not a classroom, not a curriculum — is the richest learning environment your kids will ever encounter. That's it.

It doesn't require full-time travel. It doesn't require a van. It doesn't require quitting your job or pulling your kids out of school tomorrow.

### What it looks like in practice

Worldschooling exists on a spectrum, and every point on that spectrum is valid.

**Full-time travel families.** These are the ones you see online most often. They've sold or rented their homes and travel continuously. This is one version of worldschooling, but it's not the only one.

**Seasonal travelers.** Families who take extended trips — a month in Mexico over winter, a summer in Portugal, a semester abroad. The rest of the year, they're home.

**Weekend explorers.** Families who worldschool locally. They visit cultural festivals, nature preserves, farmers markets, ethnic neighborhoods, historical sites — all within driving distance. They approach their own city with the curiosity of a traveler.

**Worldschool mindset at home.** Families who never leave their zip code but see learning in everything. The grocery store becomes a lesson in economics. The backyard becomes a biology lab. Cooking dinner becomes chemistry and fractions and cultural exploration.

All of these count. All of these are worldschooling. If you're reading this and thinking, "We could never do the full-time travel thing," that's completely fine. You don't have to.

### What it isn't

Worldschooling is not a vacation with educational bits tacked on. It's not dragging reluctant kids through museums so you can feel like you're doing something academic. It's not Instagram content creation disguised as education.

It's also not the opposite of structure. It's not chaos. It's not "just let the kids run wild and hope they learn something." You can have rhythm, intention, and reflection without having a rigid plan.

And — this matters — it's not only for wealthy families. Some of the best worldschooling happens at a local park, a community garden, or a kitchen table where you're cooking a recipe from a country you've been reading about together. Money opens doors, sure. But curiosity opens more.

### Our version

I want to be honest about what our worldschooling looked like, because it wasn't the glossy version.

We weren't backpacking minimalists. We weren't in a luxury resort. We were somewhere in between — renting apartments by the month in Central America, cooking most of our meals at home, figuring out bus routes and grocery stores in Spanish we barely spoke.

Some days were magical. Whales breaching beside our boat while Zach furiously looked up migration patterns on his tablet. Julia discovering tide pools in Costa Rica and spending an hour identifying every creature inside.

Some days were terrible. Fights about math in a hot apartment. A sick kid with no nearby doctor. Me crying in a bathroom because I was sure I was ruining their education.

Both kinds of days were worldschooling. That's the part nobody tells you.

> **The world has been teaching children since long before classrooms existed. Worldschooling isn't a new invention. It's a return to something very old.**

### Reflection prompts

- When you imagine worldschooling for your family, what does it look like? (Be honest — not aspirational.)
- Where on the spectrum do you fall right now: full-time travel, seasonal, weekend explorer, or mindset at home?
- What's one thing your kids are naturally curious about that the real world could teach better than a book?

## Chapter 2: Is Your Family Ready? (A Gentle Honest Check)

### This isn't a quiz

I'm not going to score your readiness on a scale of one to ten. Readiness isn't a number — it's a conversation. Mostly with yourself.

Some families are ready for a two-week trial trip. Some need a year of preparation before they leave their city. Some are never going to travel full-time, and they're going to worldschool beautifully from home. All of those are fine.

What matters is that you're asking the question honestly.

### Questions worth sitting with

These aren't trick questions. There are no right answers. But your honest responses will tell you a lot about what kind of worldschooling fits your family right now.

**About your kids:**
- How do your kids handle change? Do they adapt quickly, or do they need long runways?
- What are they obsessed with right now? Could a trip or experience feed that obsession?
- How do they handle boredom? (This one matters more than you'd think.)
- If they're currently in school, how long have they been there? Have you thought about deschooling time?

**About you:**
- Can you sit with uncertainty? When your kids aren't producing visible "work," how does that feel in your body?
- Are you doing this because you genuinely want to, or because you feel like you should?
- What's your relationship with control? Worldschooling requires letting go of a lot of it.
- How do you handle being uncomfortable? Because you will be.

**About your situation:**
- Is your partner on board? (More on this in a minute.)
- What does your financial picture look like — honestly, without judgment?
- Do you have legal obligations around homeschool reporting in your state or province?
- What would need to change for this to be possible? Is that change realistic?

### Ages and stages

Worldschooling looks different at every age, and that's a feature, not a problem.

**Toddlers and preschoolers (0-5).** Everything is already worldschooling at this age. Toddlers learn through sensory experience, and travel — even local exploration — is one giant sensory buffet. The challenge isn't education. It's naps, snacks, and meltdowns in public. Plan around their needs, not around sightseeing.

**Elementary age (6-10).** The sweet spot for many families. Kids this age are curious about everything, flexible enough to adapt to new environments, and old enough to remember what they experience. Julia was seven when we started, and she absorbed every new place like a sponge. This is the age when following their interests can lead to the most surprising deep dives.

**Tweens (10-13).** Still deeply curious, but more opinionated about what interests them. Zach at ten had strong preferences — he wanted engineering, animals, and water sports. He did not want art museums. We learned to negotiate. Tweens can also take more ownership of their learning: blogs, journals, research projects, videos.

**Teens (14-18).** More logistically complex if you're thinking about transcripts, college applications, or career preparation. But also the age when worldschooling can be most transformative. A teenager who spends a summer volunteering in another country, learning a language through immersion, or apprenticing with a local craftsperson is building a life resume, not just an academic one.

### The partner conversation

If you have a partner, this section matters.

You might be the one reading this guide, burning with excitement, ready to book flights tonight. Your partner might be the one asking reasonable questions about health insurance and retirement savings. Both of you are right.

The worst thing you can do is steamroll. The second worst thing is give up without really talking about it.

Here's what worked for us: I didn't start with "Let's sell everything and travel." I started with "What if we tried one month?" A trial period feels less like a life-altering commitment and more like an experiment. Experiments are easier to agree to.

If your partner is hesitant, listen to what they're actually worried about. Usually it's not the travel itself — it's the fear underneath. Fear about money. Fear about the kids falling behind. Fear about what their parents will think. Those fears are real and deserve real answers, not dismissal.

And if the answer is "not right now"? That's okay. Worldschooling will still be here when you're ready.

### The financial reality check

I'm going to be straight with you: I can't tell you what worldschooling will cost your family, because the range is enormous. Some families spend less traveling in Southeast Asia or Central America than they would staying home and paying a mortgage. Others burn through savings at a pace that's not sustainable.

What I can tell you is this — sit down with real numbers before you commit. Not fantasy numbers. Not "we'll figure it out" numbers. Real ones.

What are your current monthly expenses? What would those look like on the road? What income sources will you have? What's your emergency fund?

This isn't about having a certain amount of money. It's about knowing your numbers so you can make clear-eyed decisions. Chapter 8 goes deeper into budget-friendly approaches, but the honest check starts here.

### When the answer is "not yet"

If you've read through this chapter and you're feeling a quiet "not yet" — I want you to hear this clearly: that's not a failure. That's wisdom.

Not yet means you're being honest about where your family is. Not yet means you're choosing readiness over recklessness. Not yet means you can start worldschooling at home, in your own way, this weekend, while you prepare for whatever comes next.

The families who rush into this before they're ready often burn out fastest. The ones who take the time to get aligned, save up, deschool their kids (and themselves), and plan with intention? They're the ones who call it the best thing they ever did.

### The deschooling principle

One more thing before we move on. If your kids are currently in traditional school or have recently left, please don't jump straight from a classroom to a plane and expect magic.

Your family needs deschooling time first. That means a period — usually about a month for every year your child spent in school — where you step away from anything that feels like academics and just let everyone decompress.

I know that feels counterintuitive. You might be thinking, "We only have six months — we can't waste two of them doing nothing!" But deschooling isn't nothing. It's the reset that makes everything else work. A child who hasn't deschooled will resist real-world learning because they're still measuring everything against the school model.

If you want to go deeper on this, my Deschooling Guide walks through the whole process. But the short version is: give it time. Trust the process. The learning will come.

> **"Not yet" is not "never." It's "I'm getting ready." And getting ready is its own kind of brave.**

### Reflection prompts

- What came up for you in the honest-check questions? Any surprises?
- If your family isn't ready for a big trip, what's one small worldschooling experience you could try this month?
- What's the fear underneath the fear? (It's usually not about logistics.)

## Chapter 3: The Logistics Nobody Talks About

### The unglamorous stuff that makes it all work

Here's the thing about those beautiful worldschooling accounts online: nobody posts about the two hours they spent on hold with their health insurance company. Nobody shares the story about the bank card that got declined in a foreign country at 9 PM with two hungry kids.

But that stuff matters. It matters a lot. So let's talk about it plainly.

### Health coverage abroad

This is the question I get asked most, and it's the one with the least satisfying answer: it depends on where you live, where you're going, and how long you'll be gone.

**If you're from the US:** Most domestic health insurance doesn't cover you abroad. You'll likely want a travel medical insurance policy or an expat health plan. Travel medical insurance is cheaper and covers emergencies — think broken bones, sudden illness, hospital visits. Expat plans are more comprehensive and more expensive, closer to regular health insurance.

**If you're from Canada:** Your provincial health plan may cover emergencies abroad for a limited time, but coverage varies by province and it's often less than you'd think. Supplemental travel insurance is strongly recommended.

**What we did:** We carried travel medical insurance for the whole family. It cost a fraction of what we'd been paying for domestic coverage, and it covered us in every country we visited. We also kept a small emergency fund specifically for health costs.

**Practical tip:** Before you leave, get everyone's dental work and checkups done. Get prescriptions filled for more than you think you'll need. Carry a basic first-aid kit. And research where the nearest quality medical facility is in every place you stay. You'll probably never need it. But knowing calms the part of your brain that worries.

### Visas and length of stay

Most countries allow tourists to stay for 60 to 90 days without a special visa. For many worldschooling families, that's plenty — you can spend a month or two in a country and move on.

If you want to stay longer, research the specific visa requirements for your destination. Some countries offer extended tourist visas, digital nomad visas, or student visas. Some allow "visa runs" — leaving the country briefly and re-entering for a fresh tourist stamp. (This is common but research the legality for your specific destination. Rules change.)

**What we learned:** Don't overstay a visa. Ever. It's not worth the risk. Plan your stays within the legal limits and build travel days into your rhythm. We found that 3-4 weeks in one place was our sweet spot anyway — long enough to settle in, short enough to stay curious.

### Managing money across borders

This is one of those things that sounds complicated but is mostly just annoying.

**Bank cards.** Tell your bank you're traveling before you leave. Get a card with no foreign transaction fees if you can. We used a combination of a debit card and a credit card that didn't charge fees on international purchases.

**Cash vs. card.** This depends entirely on where you go. In Costa Rica, cards were accepted almost everywhere. In parts of Panama and El Salvador, cash was essential. Always carry some local currency.

**Exchange rates.** Don't exchange money at airports — the rates are terrible. Use ATMs for local currency (your bank may charge a small fee, but the exchange rate is usually better). Avoid currency exchange booths unless you have no other option.

**Budgeting across currencies.** I kept a simple spreadsheet with our daily spending in both local currency and our home currency. Nothing fancy. Just enough to know where we stood at the end of each week.

### Internet and connectivity

If you or your partner work remotely, this is non-negotiable. If your kids maintain a blog or create videos (like ours do), it matters too.

**Research connectivity before you book accommodation.** Ask about Wi-Fi speed. Read reviews that mention internet quality. In some places, the listing says "Wi-Fi included" and what they mean is "Wi-Fi exists, technically, if you stand in one specific corner of the kitchen."

**Backup plan.** Get a local SIM card with data in every country. They're cheap and available at convenience stores or phone shops in most places. Mobile data saved us more than once when the apartment Wi-Fi went down.

**Adjust expectations.** Some places will have lightning-fast fiber. Some won't. Build flexibility into your work and your kids' projects. This is, incidentally, a great real-world lesson for everyone.

### Mail and legal address

You still need a mailing address back home. Bills arrive, government documents show up, insurance companies want a physical address.

Options: a trusted family member or friend who will forward important mail, a mail forwarding service, or a PO box with scanning capabilities (several companies offer this — they open your mail, scan it, and email you the contents).

This is boring but important. Set it up before you leave so it's one less thing to think about on the road.

### The house question

What do you do with your home while you're gone?

**Keep it.** If you own and can afford the mortgage while traveling, this is the simplest option. You have a guaranteed place to come home to. You might ask a friend or family member to check in periodically.

**Rent it.** Your home can fund your travel. Long-term rentals are less hassle than short-term. A property manager can handle tenant issues so you're not getting maintenance calls from another time zone. The income might cover your mortgage and part of your travel expenses.

**Sell it.** The biggest commitment and the biggest freedom. Some families do this and don't regret it. Some do and miss having a home base. Only you know which camp you'd fall into.

**If you rent your home:** Talk to your landlord. Some leases allow subletting. Others don't. Don't assume.

### What worked for us, what didn't

I'll be honest: the logistics were the part that stressed me out most before we left. More than the education stuff, more than the parenting stuff. I lost sleep over health insurance forms and visa timelines.

And then we left, and most of it sorted itself out faster than I expected. The bank cards worked. The travel insurance was straightforward. The apartments had Wi-Fi.

What caught us off guard: the mental load of constantly figuring things out in a new place. Where's the grocery store? How does the bus system work? Is this water safe to drink? Each of these is small. Together, they're exhausting. Give yourself grace during the first few days in any new place. It gets easier.

The single best logistics decision we made was slowing down. When you're only in a place for three days, everything feels frantic. When you're there for three weeks, you find your rhythm. You find your grocery store. You know which café has the best Wi-Fi. You stop being a tourist and start being a temporary local. That's when everything — the learning, the logistics, the family dynamic — clicks into place.

> **Handle the logistics once, live the experience for months. Don't let paperwork keep you from the life you want.**

### Reflection prompts

- Which logistical question feels most daunting to you right now? What's one step you could take this week to answer it?
- Have you looked into health coverage options for your family abroad?
- What would slowing down look like for your family — could you stay somewhere for weeks instead of days?

## Chapter 4: Packing for Learning (Less Is More)

### The myth of the mobile classroom

Before we left, I spent an embarrassing amount of time assembling what I called our "travel learning kit." I had workbooks for each kid. Flashcards. A math manipulatives set. Colored pencils in a labeled case. A laminated multiplication chart.

I was a teacher. I knew how classrooms worked. I was going to make this work on the road.

Here's what actually happened: I dragged that bag through the Miami airport, the Panama City airport, and a taxi with no trunk space. The kids opened exactly one workbook — once — in the first week. By week three, I stuffed the whole lot into a donation bin at a hostel in Boquete, Panama, and felt lighter in every way.

The less stuff you carry, the better. This is true for luggage, and it's true for learning.

### What the world provides

Here's what I wish someone had told me before I packed all those supplies: the world already has everything your kids need to learn.

**Libraries.** Almost every city has one, and many welcome visitors. We found English-language books in unexpected places.

**Museums.** Not just the big famous ones. Small local museums, natural history collections, interactive science centers. Many have free or reduced admission days.

**Nature.** This is the most underrated classroom on earth. Tide pools, hiking trails, rivers, forests, beaches, even a city park — all of it is teeming with learning. A kid crouching by a tide pool in Costa Rica is doing more real science than a kid filling in a worksheet about marine ecosystems.

**People.** Conversations with locals, shopkeepers, other travelers, other kids. Language practice, cultural exchange, perspective-building. You can't pack this in a bag.

**Markets.** Farmers markets, flea markets, street markets. Math, economics, culture, negotiation, sensory exploration — all happening naturally.

**Problems to solve.** Navigating a bus system in a foreign language. Converting currencies. Reading a map. Figuring out how to ask for directions when you don't speak the language. These real-world problems teach more than any textbook.

### What to actually bring

After seven months on the road, here's what I'd tell a friend to pack for learning:

**A device per kid (or shared).** Loaded with books, a journal app, a browser, and whatever creative tools they use. Zach uses his tablet for research and writing. Julia uses hers for drawing and video editing. A tablet or laptop is your entire mobile library, writing desk, and creative studio.

**A physical journal per kid.** Some kids prefer writing or sketching by hand. Julia's nature journal from our trip is one of our family's most treasured objects. A simple notebook and a few good pens are enough.

**A small art supply kit.** Emphasis on small. A travel watercolor set, a handful of colored pencils, a glue stick. Julia could create for hours with these basics. You don't need the full craft aisle.

**A camera or phone.** Documentation is learning (more on this later), and kids who photograph or video their experiences process them differently than kids who just pass through.

**That's basically it.**

No workbooks. No flashcards. No laminated charts. No curriculum guides.

I know this feels radical, especially if you're coming from a traditional school environment. But think about it this way: when your family takes a vacation and your kids come home buzzing about everything they saw and did and tasted and discovered, you didn't bring workbooks on that trip either. Worldschooling is just giving yourself permission to trust that same kind of learning on a longer timeline.

### The Anywhere Learning philosophy in action

This is the core of everything I believe about education, and it's the reason I started Anywhere Learning: you don't need special materials, prep, or planning to help your kids learn meaningful things. You need presence, curiosity, and a willingness to follow where the moment leads.

When Zach stood at the edge of the Panama Canal and asked, "How does this actually work?" — that was the beginning of weeks of deep learning. I didn't plan it. I didn't have a lesson prepped. I just said, "I don't know. Let's find out."

When Julia sat on a beach sorting shells by size, color, and type for two hours — that was categorization, pattern recognition, planning, and decision-making. Nobody assigned it. She was just doing what came naturally.

The learning is already happening. Your job isn't to create it. It's to notice it, support it, and get out of the way.

> **Pack light. Trust your kids. The world has more teaching materials than any supply store ever will.**

### Reflection prompts

- What are you tempted to pack that you probably don't need?
- What does your child naturally reach for when they're curious about something — a book, a device, a sketchbook, their hands?
- Could you try one screen-free, supply-free learning day this week and see what happens?

## Chapter 5: How Learning Actually Happens on the Road

### Finding your family's rhythm

I use the word rhythm instead of schedule for a reason. A schedule says "math at 9, reading at 10, science at 11." A rhythm says "we have a shape to our days, but it breathes."

On the road, our family rhythm looked something like this:

**Morning anchor.** We woke up without alarms (usually). Everyone had quiet time — reading, drawing, writing, whatever they wanted. This was our grounding time. Zach often read or worked on his blog. Julia drew or watched nature documentaries. I had coffee and planned the day loosely. This lasted an hour or two, and it was sacred. Nobody rushed it.

**Exploration.** This was the heart of the day. A hike, a market, a beach, a town to explore, a museum, a cooking experiment. Sometimes we had a destination. Sometimes we just walked and saw what happened. The exploration wasn't "the field trip." It was the whole point.

**Afternoon downtime.** After a full morning out, everyone needed to recharge. The kids went to their own corners — devices, books, art, naps. This is the time that looks unproductive from the outside but is actually crucial. Processing time is learning time. Some of the best questions Zach and Julia ever asked came during quiet afternoons, hours after the experience that sparked them.

**Evening reflection.** Not every night, but often, we'd talk about the day over dinner. What surprised you? What did you notice? What do you want to learn more about? This wasn't formal. It was just conversation. But it turned experiences into understanding.

That was it. That was our "curriculum."

### Seeing learning everywhere

This is the skill that changes everything, and it takes practice.

When your kids calculate the tip at a restaurant in a foreign currency — that's math. When they order food in broken Spanish — that's language acquisition. When they notice that the cliffs are crumbling into the ocean — that's geology. When they barter with a vendor at a market — that's economics and communication.

In Costa Rica, we took a boat ride through a mangrove. Zach and Julia spent the entire trip identifying monkey species with a field guide. They spotted a crocodile half-hidden in the mud. They watched cliffs eroding into the water and asked why. Later that same trip, whales breached right beside our boat, and we spent the evening reading about humpback migration patterns.

None of that was on a lesson plan. All of it was real-world science, geography, and research — happening because the kids were there, paying attention, and curious.

The trick is learning to see it. When you come from a school background (as I did), you're trained to think learning looks like sitting, writing, and answering questions. Unlearning that is one of the most important things you'll do as a worldschooling parent. Learning looks like a kid crouching in the mud staring at a crab. Learning looks like a twelve-year-old Googling "how do canal locks work" at 10 PM because he can't stop thinking about it. Learning looks like a nine-year-old spending three hours sorting seashells.

### Following your child's obsessions

This might be the most powerful principle in worldschooling: when a child becomes obsessed with something, get out of the way and let it happen.

Zach's Panama Canal phase lasted weeks. He researched the engineering. He learned about the history — how many workers died building it, the political conflicts around it, the expansion project. He wrote about it in French (his second language) because he wanted to challenge himself. He drew cross-section diagrams. Nobody assigned any of this. He was just captivated.

If I had been following a curriculum, we would have spent one day on the Panama Canal and moved on to the next chapter. Instead, Zach went deep. He learned more about engineering, history, geography, and writing from that single obsession than he would have from months of textbook chapters.

Julia's obsessions look different. She draws everything. She creates YouTube video thumbnails. She went through a phase of making hurricane posters — researching categories, wind speeds, tracking patterns. Again, nobody assigned it.

Your child's obsession might be dinosaurs, cooking, bugs, skateboarding, or Minecraft. It doesn't matter what it is. What matters is that you give it space, take it seriously, and help them go deeper when they want to.

### Documentation as learning

One of the most valuable things our kids do is document their experiences. Zach and Julia write a travel blog on Google Sites and make YouTube videos about their travels.

This started as a way to share our experiences with family back home. It became so much more. Writing a blog post requires organizing your thoughts, choosing what matters, explaining things clearly, and revising your work. Making a video requires planning, scripting, filming, editing, and storytelling. These are real skills — communication, technology, creativity, project management — and the kids are learning them because they want to, not because they have to.

Documentation can be whatever fits your child: a journal, a sketchbook, a photo collection, a podcast, a scrapbook, social media posts, a video diary. The format matters less than the practice of reflecting on what you've experienced and turning it into something to share.

### The "unproductive" day

Let me tell you about a day in Costa Rica that I almost counted as a failure.

We didn't go anywhere. The kids slept in. Zach watched YouTube videos about engineering for three hours. Julia drew in her journal and then wandered around the yard poking at bugs. I read a book. Nobody produced anything that looked like schoolwork.

That evening, Zach came to dinner and explained, in detail, how suspension bridges distribute weight. Julia showed us a drawing of every insect she'd found in the yard, labeled with descriptions she'd looked up.

The "unproductive" day was full of learning. I just couldn't see it while it was happening because I was still measuring against the school model — visible effort, tangible output, progress you can point to.

After a full day of exploring, everyone needs downtime. And some of the best questions, the deepest thinking, and the most creative work comes during that quiet space. Protect it.

### Slow travel wins

If I could give worldschooling families one piece of practical advice, it would be this: stay longer in fewer places.

When you're rushing from city to city, spending two or three days in each spot, you never get past the tourist experience. You're constantly orienting — where's the grocery store, how does the bus work, which neighborhood is safe — and by the time you've figured it out, you're packing up again.

When you stay somewhere for three or four weeks, everything changes. You become a temporary local. You find your favorite café, your walking route, the park where the neighborhood kids play. Your kids make friends. You start to understand the place, not just see it.

The deepest learning happened for us during our longest stays. The places we rushed through are a blur. The places where we slowed down became part of us.

> **Don't mistake movement for learning. The richest education often happens when you stop moving and start noticing.**

### Reflection prompts

- What does your family's natural rhythm look like? When are your kids most energized, most creative, most reflective?
- What is your child obsessed with right now? How could you help them go deeper?
- Could you try the morning anchor + exploration + reflection pattern this week, even at home?

## Chapter 6: Keeping Records While Moving

### Why you might need to (and why it's not as hard as you think)

Let's talk about the elephant in the room: documentation and record-keeping.

Depending on where you live, you may have legal requirements for homeschool reporting. Some states and provinces require portfolios, assessments, or progress reports. Others require almost nothing. Know your local laws before you leave — a quick search or a call to your local homeschool association can clarify this in minutes.

Beyond legal requirements, there are two other reasons you might want to keep records:

**Your own peace of mind.** On the hard days — and there will be hard days — it helps to look back at a collection of evidence showing that learning is actually happening. A photo log, a folder of your child's work, a list of places explored and books read. Not for anyone else. For the voice in your head that occasionally whispers, "Are they learning enough?"

**The relatives.** Grandma wants to know the kids are getting an education. Uncle Dave has opinions about math. Having something concrete to show — not to prove yourself, but to ease the conversation — can reduce a lot of family tension.

### Simple approaches that work

You don't need a complicated tracking system. You need something sustainable — something you'll actually do when you're tired and it's 9 PM and you'd rather read a book.

**Photos.** Take a photo every day of something your kids learned, made, explored, or discovered. At the end of a month, you'll have a visual portfolio that tells a powerful story. Put them in a folder on your phone. That's it.

**A weekly family journal.** Once a week, spend fifteen minutes writing down what happened that week. Where you went. What the kids were interested in. What they made, read, or asked about. Keep it short. A few bullet points per day is enough.

**Your child's own documentation.** If your kids blog, journal, draw, or make videos, that's your record. Zach and Julia's travel blog is a better portfolio than anything I could have assembled on their behalf. It's in their own words, about what mattered to them.

**Project evidence.** When a child does a deep dive — like Zach's Panama Canal research — save the results. The writing, the drawings, the diagrams. Print or screenshot the blog posts. These aren't just records. They're proof that self-directed learning produces real, substantial work.

### How Anywhere Learning guides help

This is where I'll be transparent about what I do and why.

The activity guides I create at Anywhere Learning exist for exactly the moments when you want a little more structure. Maybe you've been free-flowing for a few weeks and you want a framework for the day. Maybe you need to document a specific subject area for your state's requirements. Maybe you just want a starting point that's easy to follow.

Every guide is designed to be opened on any device and followed along — no prep, no special materials. They're flexible enough to adjust for different ages and abilities, and reusable year after year. They're not a curriculum. They're a tool you pull out when you want it and put away when you don't.

I made them because, during our travels, I kept wishing for exactly this — something simple I could use on a low-energy day or when the kids needed a different kind of stimulation. Not a workbook. Not a lesson plan. Just a guide that turns an ordinary moment into a meaningful learning experience.

### Don't over-document

One more thing: don't let documentation become the point.

I've seen families so focused on capturing and logging everything that they stop actually experiencing it. They're photographing the tide pool instead of crouching down and looking inside it. They're writing in the journal instead of having the conversation.

Live it first. Capture it second. A rough photo and a few scribbled notes at the end of the day are worth more than a perfectly curated portfolio that took three hours to assemble while your kids played alone.

> **The record is not the learning. The learning is the learning. Keep it simple.**

## Chapter 7: The Hard Parts (Nobody Posts These on Instagram)

### The beautiful-messy truth

I need to write this chapter because if I only told you the good parts — the whales, the tide pools, the Panama Canal obsession — I'd be doing you a disservice. I'd be building the same unrealistic picture that makes families feel like failures when their experience doesn't match the highlight reel.

Worldschooling is wonderful. It's also hard. Both of those things are true at the same time.

### Homesickness

Yours will probably hit first. It comes at unexpected moments — seeing a kitchen that looks like your mom's, hearing a song from home, missing the way the light looks in your own bedroom at 7 AM.

The kids' homesickness is different. It might show up as crankiness, resistance, or a sudden obsession with what their friends back home are doing. Julia went through a phase where she asked to FaceTime her best friend every single day. Zach got quiet for a week and then told us he missed his bedroom.

You can't fix homesickness. You can acknowledge it. "I miss home too. And it's okay to feel that." You can create connections — video calls, letters, sending photos to grandparents. You can also just sit with it. It passes. And it often comes back. That's normal.

### Missing friends and community

This is one of the hardest parts, especially for kids who had strong friendships.

Traveling families can be surprisingly isolated. You're surrounded by people, but you're always the newcomer. You make friends and then leave. Or they leave. The kids get close to someone at a playground and then you're on a bus the next morning.

What helps: staying places long enough to form connections. Seeking out other traveling families through online communities, co-working spaces, or homeschool meetups abroad (they exist in more places than you'd think). Maintaining old friendships through regular video calls. And being honest with your kids: "Making friends and saying goodbye is part of this. It's hard, and it's also a skill you're building."

### The fight about math at a café in a foreign country

It happens. I promise you, it happens to every worldschooling family.

There will be a day when you decide it's time for some focused math practice, and your kid decides it absolutely is not. And this fight will happen in a beautiful café in a beautiful country, and you will feel ridiculous and frustrated and like you're failing at everything.

Here's what I learned: the fight is almost never about math. It's about power, or exhaustion, or the fact that pulling out a math problem in the middle of a trip feels jarring and arbitrary — because it is. The math at the restaurant (tip calculations, currency conversion) felt natural. The math at the café table felt like school.

You don't have to have this figured out perfectly. Some families do morning focused work and it works beautifully. Others weave everything into the day and skip the sit-down sessions entirely. Find what works for yours, and be willing to adjust when it stops working.

### When a kid hates a place you love

This happened to us more than once. I'd be enchanted by a village or a beach or a market, and one of the kids would be miserable. Too hot. Too boring. Too many bugs. Want to go back to the apartment.

You have two choices: force them through it (which guarantees everyone has a bad time) or adapt. We learned to negotiate. "Let's stay for one more hour and then head back." "What would make this better for you?" Sometimes the answer was ice cream. Sometimes it was giving them their device and letting them sit in the shade while we explored. Flexibility isn't giving up. It's parenting.

### Loneliness

This one's for you, the parent.

You might feel lonely in ways you don't expect. You're far from your own friends. Your partner (if you have one) is dealing with their own adjustment. The other parents you meet are passing through. You might miss having someone who really knows you — not travel-you, but everyday-you.

This is real, and it matters. Maintain your connections back home. Find online communities of worldschooling families (there are several good ones). And talk about it with your partner or a friend. Loneliness on the road doesn't mean you made the wrong choice. It means you're human.

### Health scares far from home

We were lucky. We never had a serious medical emergency while traveling. But there were fevers, stomach bugs, and one alarming allergic reaction that had me on the phone with a nurse line at 2 AM.

Being far from your regular doctor, your familiar hospital, your pharmacy where everyone knows your name — that's scary. Especially with kids.

What helps: the health preparation I mentioned in Chapter 3. Know where to go before you need to go there. Carry basic medications. Have your travel insurance information on your phone, not buried in an email somewhere. And remember that medical care exists everywhere. People raise children all over the world, and the vast majority of health situations are manageable with local care.

### The guilt

This one is sneaky, and it might be the hardest part of all.

Guilt about spending money on travel instead of saving for their college fund. Guilt about pulling them away from stability. Guilt when someone back home makes a comment about the kids "falling behind." Guilt on the hard days when you think, "Did I do this for them, or for me?"

I felt all of it. Here's what I know now: the guilt is the old system talking. It's the voice that says education has to look a certain way, stability has to mean one place, and good parenting means sacrifice, not adventure.

You're not ruining your kids. You're showing them that the world is bigger than one classroom, one neighborhood, one way of living. That's a gift. Even on the days when it doesn't feel like one.

> **The hard parts don't mean you're doing it wrong. They mean you're doing something real.**

### Reflection prompts

- Which hard part concerns you most? What would help you feel prepared for it?
- Who in your life can you be honest with about the difficult moments — not just the beautiful ones?
- What's the guilt telling you? And is it true?

## Chapter 8: Worldschooling on a Budget

### It doesn't have to cost a fortune

Let me challenge an assumption: worldschooling might be cheaper than your current life.

I know that sounds counterintuitive. Travel is expensive, right? Hotels, flights, restaurants, tourist attractions — it adds up fast. That's true if you travel like a tourist. But worldschooling families don't travel like tourists.

When you slow down, rent apartments instead of booking hotels, cook at home instead of eating out, and choose destinations with a lower cost of living — the math often works out in your favor. Families living in high-cost areas of the US or Canada sometimes spend less per month traveling in Central America, Southeast Asia, or Southern Europe than they would at home.

This isn't guaranteed. It depends entirely on your circumstances, your destination choices, and your spending habits. But "we can't afford it" isn't always as true as it feels.

### Lower-cost accommodation strategies

**Long-term rentals.** Booking an apartment for a month is dramatically cheaper than booking it by the night. In many countries, monthly rentals are half (or less) the per-night rate. Facebook groups, local rental sites, and word-of-mouth are often better than Airbnb for longer stays.

**House sitting.** Platforms exist where homeowners need someone to care for their home and pets while they travel. In exchange, you stay for free. We met families who house-sat their way through Europe for months without paying for a single night of accommodation.

**Home exchange.** Trade your home with a family in another location. You stay in theirs, they stay in yours. Both families save on housing costs, and your home doesn't sit empty.

**Hostels with family rooms.** Not every hostel is a backpacker party. Many offer private family rooms at a fraction of hotel prices. Bonus: built-in community. Your kids will meet other kids in the common areas.

**Negotiate.** In many parts of the world, especially for longer stays, prices are flexible. Ask for a discount. Ask if there's a monthly rate. The worst they can say is no.

### Food on a budget

Cooking at home is cheaper everywhere on the planet, and it's also a worldschooling opportunity. Take your kids to the local market or grocery store. Let them pick unfamiliar ingredients. Cook together. A meal prepared from local ingredients in a foreign kitchen teaches more about culture than a museum audioguide.

When you do eat out, look for where locals eat — not where tourists eat. The food is better and the prices are lower. In Central America, we ate at "sodas" (small local restaurants) for a fraction of what tourist restaurants charged, and the food was incredible.

Street food is another option — often cheap, delicious, and a cultural experience in itself. Use your judgment about food safety, but don't be afraid. Millions of people eat street food every day.

### Free learning everywhere

The best worldschooling experiences are almost always free.

Nature costs nothing. Beaches, trails, rivers, forests, mountains, parks — free. A morning spent exploring tide pools or identifying birds in a forest is richer than any paid attraction.

Walking is free. Walk through neighborhoods, markets, historic areas, waterfronts. Look up. Look down. Talk to people. Stop when something catches your eye.

Public spaces are free. Plazas, playgrounds, community events, street performers, public art — all free.

Libraries are free. Many cities welcome visitors. It's a place to read, rest, and connect with the local community.

Conversations are free. Talking to a fisherman about his catch, a farmer about her crops, a shopkeeper about his neighborhood — these interactions are worldschooling at its purest.

The most expensive day of our entire trip was probably the least educational. The days that cost almost nothing were often the most memorable.

### Working while traveling

Many worldschooling families fund their travels through remote work. This is more realistic now than it's ever been. Freelance writing, design, programming, virtual assistance, online teaching, consulting — the list of location-independent work continues to grow.

Some families build a location-independent income before they leave. Others transition gradually, keeping one foot in their old career while building remote work on the side. Others save up and travel on savings with a return-to-work deadline.

There's no single right approach. What matters is having a realistic plan, not a fantasy. "We'll figure it out on the road" is not a financial plan.

### The budget conversation

If you have a partner, sit down together with real numbers. Not vague concepts — actual numbers. What you earn, what you spend, what you have saved, what your monthly minimum would be on the road.

This conversation isn't romantic. It might cause an argument. Have it anyway. Financial stress is one of the top reasons worldschooling families come home early. The families who make it work financially aren't necessarily the ones with the most money. They're the ones who were honest with each other about the numbers before they left.

> **Worldschooling on a budget isn't about deprivation. It's about choosing experiences over expenses. The best learning doesn't come with a price tag.**

### Reflection prompts

- What does your family spend per month right now? Could you spend less in a different location?
- Which budget accommodation strategy appeals to you most?
- What are three free worldschooling experiences available in your area right now?

## Chapter 9: Coming Home (Or Not)

### Re-entry is a thing

Nobody warns you about this part.

You've been living in a different rhythm for weeks or months. Your kids have been learning through the world, following their curiosity, moving at their own pace. And then you come home, and everything is exactly the way you left it. The house, the neighborhood, the grocery store, the routine. But you're different.

Re-entry can be disorienting. It was for us. The kids were restless. I felt claustrophobic. Everything felt smaller. Not bad — just smaller. Like putting on shoes that used to fit perfectly but now feel a little tight.

### What to expect

**Your kids might struggle to reconnect with old friends.** Their friends have continued in school, had their own experiences, and moved on. Your kids have had experiences that are hard to explain to someone who wasn't there. This isn't permanent. It just takes time.

**You might mourn the rhythm you had.** The morning anchor, the exploration, the wide-open days. Coming back to a more structured life can feel like a loss, even if you chose it.

**The relatives will have opinions.** "So are the kids all caught up?" "Are they going back to school now?" "Was it worth it?" You'll need to decide how much energy you want to spend on these conversations.

**You might want to leave again immediately.** This is normal. Give yourself time to settle before making big decisions.

### How worldschooling changes your homeschooling at home

Here's the beautiful thing about worldschooling: you can't unsee it.

Once you've watched your kids learn from the world, you can't go back to believing that learning only happens at a desk. You'll walk past a construction site and see a physics lesson. You'll cook dinner and see chemistry. You'll overhear your kids negotiating with each other and see communication, conflict resolution, and empathy in action.

The worldschool mindset doesn't require a passport. It requires a shift in how you see ordinary life. And that shift is permanent.

After we came home, our homeschooling looked completely different. Less structured. More responsive. More trusting of the kids' natural curiosity. We still had our daily rhythm — morning reading time, afternoon free time, evening conversation. But the content came from life, not from a curriculum.

A trip to the farmers market became an economics lesson. A rainy afternoon became a documentary marathon that led to a week-long research project. A broken appliance became an engineering challenge. The learning was everywhere, just like it had been on the road.

### Keeping the mindset without the travel

You don't need a plane ticket to worldschool. I'll keep saying this until it sticks.

Worldschooling at home looks like:

- Exploring your own city like a tourist — visiting neighborhoods you've never been to, trying restaurants from cultures you're curious about, finding hidden parks and trails
- Following your child's interests wherever they lead, even if it doesn't look like "school"
- Using real-world experiences as the foundation for learning — grocery shopping, cooking, gardening, building, fixing, exploring
- Traveling locally — day trips, weekend camping, visiting nearby towns
- Connecting with your community — volunteering, joining local clubs, attending cultural events
- Staying curious together as a family

The physical travel is wonderful. But the philosophy is what matters. And the philosophy works anywhere.

### When the trip ends but the philosophy doesn't

This is what Anywhere Learning is about, at its core.

The guides I create aren't about travel. They're about seeing learning in the everyday. Opening a guide on your phone while you're at a park, a beach, a kitchen table, or a backyard, and turning an ordinary moment into something meaningful. No prep. No special materials. Just you, your kids, and whatever the world puts in front of you.

The trip might end. The worldschooling doesn't have to.

> **You don't need a plane ticket to worldschool. You need permission to see learning everywhere.**

## Chapter 10: Your First Trip — A Practical Starter Framework

### Start small

If you've made it this far, you might be feeling a pull to do something. Good. Let's channel that into action.

But please don't start with a seven-month trip to Central America. We did that, and while I don't regret it, I also acknowledge we did it the hard way. If we'd started smaller, we would have made fewer mistakes and felt more confident sooner.

Start with a weekend. Seriously. A two-day trip to a nearby town, a state park, or even an unfamiliar neighborhood in your own city. Approach it with intention — not as a vacation, but as a worldschooling experiment. Watch what happens. See what your kids are drawn to. Notice what kind of learning emerges when you let go of the plan.

### A one-week worldschool trial

If the weekend goes well (or even if it's messy), try a week.

**Before you go:**

Choose a destination that interests your family. Not "educational" — interesting. A coastal town. A city with great food. A place with hiking. A community with a culture different from yours. The learning will happen wherever you go, so pick somewhere everyone is excited about.

Ask your family: What are we curious about? What do we want to see, taste, try, explore? Write these down. Not as assignments — as possibilities.

**While you're there:**

Use the daily rhythm: morning anchor (quiet time, reading, creating), exploration (go out and follow your curiosity), afternoon downtime (rest, process, create), evening reflection (talk about the day).

Don't over-plan. Have one or two ideas per day, maximum. Leave room for the unexpected — that's where the best learning happens.

Let your kids lead. If they want to spend three hours at a tide pool and skip the museum, let them. The tide pool is the lesson.

**When you come home:**

Sit down as a family and talk about it. What did we learn? What surprised us? What would we do differently? What do we want to explore more?

This reflection is the most important step, and it's the one most families skip. Don't skip it.

### A one-month deep stay

Ready for more? A month in one place is where worldschooling really starts to shine.

**Choose your destination with intention.** Where does your family have a natural curiosity? If your kids are into marine biology, choose a coastal town. If they love history, choose a city with layers of it. If they want to learn Spanish, choose a Spanish-speaking country. The overlap between your family's interests and what the place offers is where the magic happens.

**Book accommodation for the full month.** You'll get better rates, and more importantly, you'll settle in. You'll stop being tourists and start being temporary residents. This is when the real learning begins.

**Establish your rhythm early.** Within the first few days, find your grocery store, your morning café, your favorite walking route, the park where kids play. Create a sense of home. Structure enables freedom.

**Follow the obsessions.** When your child gets interested in something, feed it. Take them to see it again. Help them find books, videos, or local experts. Let them spend a week on one thing instead of forcing variety. Depth beats breadth, every time.

**Document in whatever way works.** Photos, journals, blogs, videos. Don't force it, but encourage it. At the end of the month, your family will have a rich collection of evidence that learning was happening every single day.

### A simple pre-trip framework

Here's a framework you can use for any trip, whether it's a weekend or a year:

**Step 1: Interests inventory.** What is each family member curious about right now? List everything — no filtering.

**Step 2: Place research.** What does your destination offer? Nature, culture, history, food, language, art, science, industry? List everything.

**Step 3: Find the overlap.** Where do your family's interests and the place's offerings intersect? These are your starting points — not assignments, just possibilities.

**Step 4: Hold it loosely.** You'll discover things you never planned for. The framework is a starting point, not a contract. Be willing to abandon the plan when something better presents itself.

### The daily rhythm template

Use this as a starting point and adjust it to fit your family:

**Morning anchor (1-2 hours).** Quiet, self-directed time. Reading, drawing, writing, thinking. Everyone does their own thing. This is sacred — protect it.

**Exploration (2-4 hours).** Go out. Explore. Follow curiosity. One general idea for the day is enough. Leave room for spontaneity.

**Lunch and downtime (2-3 hours).** Eat. Rest. Process. Play. Be bored. Some of the best learning happens here when you're not trying.

**Afternoon flex (1-2 hours).** This is the time for follow-up. Looking things up. Working on a project. Practicing a skill. Or just more free time.

**Evening reflection (20-30 minutes).** Over dinner or before bed, talk about the day. What did you notice? What surprised you? What do you want to learn more about?

**That's it.** There's no math block. No science hour. No required reading log. Just a rhythm that makes space for living and learning at the same time.

### Permission to do it imperfectly

Your first trip will not go as planned. Something will go wrong. A kid will melt down. You'll get lost. The apartment will be disappointing. The Wi-Fi won't work. You'll have a moment where you think, "What are we doing?"

This is normal. This is worldschooling.

The families who do this well aren't the ones who do it perfectly. They're the ones who do it imperfectly, learn from it, and try again. Every mistake is a lesson — for you and for your kids. You're modeling resilience, adaptability, and courage. Those are worth more than a perfect trip.

Give yourself permission to figure it out as you go. That's what we did. That's what every worldschooling family does.

> **You don't need to know everything before you start. You just need to start.**

- What are your family's top three interests right now? Where could you go to explore them?
- What would it look like to give yourself permission to do this imperfectly?
